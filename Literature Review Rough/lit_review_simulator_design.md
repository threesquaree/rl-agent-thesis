# 2.1 User Simulator Design for Dialogue Reinforcement Learning

In reinforcement learning for dialogue, the user simulator occupies a unique position: it is simultaneously the training environment, the source of the reward signal, and the implicit model of the user population the agent will eventually serve. Unlike simulators in robotics or game-playing — where the dynamics are governed by well-understood physical or logical rules — dialogue simulators must approximate human conversational behaviour, a process that is stochastic, context-dependent, and shaped by latent variables such as personality, prior knowledge, and affective state. The fidelity of this approximation is not merely an engineering convenience; it is a first-order research variable that determines what behaviours an RL agent can learn, how quickly it learns them, and whether the resulting policy will generalise to real users. This section traces the development of user simulation for dialogue RL, from foundational agenda-based architectures through systematic evaluations of design choices to empirical demonstrations that simulator realism directly drives policy quality. In doing so, it establishes the theoretical and empirical context for the central contribution of this thesis: a controlled investigation of how incremental improvements in simulator fidelity affect the emergence of engagement-adaptive dialogue policies.

## 2.1.1 Foundations: Agenda-Based User Simulation

The modern paradigm of training dialogue policies against simulated users originates in the statistical spoken dialogue systems community. Schatzmann et al. (2007) established a foundational principle that continues to shape the field: user simulators must be sufficiently realistic to produce policies that transfer to real users, yet sufficiently tractable to permit large-scale RL training. Their work on error simulation for statistical dialogue systems demonstrated that even simple noise models — when appropriately calibrated to observed error patterns — could produce usable training environments for POMDP-based dialogue managers. Critically, Schatzmann et al. also raised an enduring concern: simulator-trained policies can fail when deployed with real users, because the simulator may not capture the full diversity of human behaviour. This tension between tractability and ecological validity remains unresolved and directly motivates the present work.

Li et al. (2016) introduced a user simulator for task-completion dialogues that became a widely adopted benchmark in the dialogue RL community. Their simulator operationalised user behaviour through an agenda-based architecture in which the simulated user maintains an explicit goal structure (a set of slot-value constraints representing, for example, a restaurant booking request) and an agenda of dialogue acts to communicate these constraints. At each turn, the simulator selects a dialogue act from the agenda based on the system's preceding action, updates its internal state, and optionally introduces noise through an error model that simulates ASR and NLU errors. This design imposed three structural assumptions that would prove influential: first, that user behaviour can be decomposed into a goal representation and a policy over dialogue acts conditioned on that goal; second, that the user's response at each turn depends primarily on the system's immediately preceding action (a Markov assumption over dialogue acts); and third, that variability across users is captured primarily through variation in goals and error rates rather than through variation in conversational strategy or affective response.

The Li et al. simulator was designed for slot-filling task-completion domains (restaurant booking, movie ticket purchase) and measured success through a binary task completion signal: the dialogue either achieved the user's goal or it did not. This binary success metric, combined with the agenda-based architecture, created a training environment with relatively clear credit assignment — the agent could learn that certain dialogue acts (e.g., confirming a slot value) directly advanced task completion, while others (e.g., requesting an already-provided slot) did not. The simulator's wide adoption reflected its practical utility: it was computationally lightweight, produced reproducible results, and generated sufficient behavioural diversity through goal sampling and error injection to prevent policy overfitting.

However, the assumptions underlying agenda-based simulation become problematic when the dialogue domain departs from slot-filling task completion. In museum guide dialogue, for instance, there is no fixed goal structure to satisfy, no binary success criterion, and no natural decomposition of visitor behaviour into slot-filling dialogue acts. The visitor's engagement state evolves continuously in response to content, pacing, and interactivity, and the appropriate measure of success is not whether a predefined goal was achieved but whether the visitor remained engaged, learned something, and had a satisfying experience. These differences mean that the agenda-based paradigm, while foundational, cannot be directly transferred to the engagement-driven dialogue setting of the present work. The challenge is to preserve the benefits of agenda-based simulation — tractability, reproducibility, and sufficient diversity — while replacing its task-completion assumptions with engagement-responsive dynamics.

## 2.1.2 Systematic Evaluation of Simulator Design Choices

The question of how simulator design decisions propagate to learned policy quality was addressed systematically by Shi and Yu (2019), whose work represents the most directly relevant prior investigation for the present thesis. Shi and Yu framed user simulator construction as a series of design choices — at the levels of both dialogue management and natural language generation — and evaluated how each choice affected the quality of RL-trained dialogue policies. Their central finding was that user simulators must provide learnable signals with sufficient dynamic range for agents to discover which actions improve performance. When signal ranges overlap or are ambiguous, the agent cannot reliably attribute improvements or degradations to its own actions, and credit assignment breaks down.

Shi and Yu evaluated multiple simulator variants and assessed their downstream impact on dialogue policies using both automatic metrics and human evaluations. A particularly important methodological contribution was their emphasis on evaluating simulators not only in terms of their surface-level realism (how closely they resemble human behaviour on distributional metrics) but also in terms of their *learnability* — whether the signals they produce are sufficiently structured to support policy improvement. This distinction between realism and learnability is central to understanding the results of the present thesis. A simulator can be highly realistic (in the sense that its output distributions match observed human data) yet produce signals that are too ambiguous for effective RL training. Conversely, a simulator can be stylised or exaggerated in its dynamics yet produce clear, learnable signals that enable the agent to discover effective strategies.

This tension is precisely what Bourdon (2025) observed in comparing his two simulators. Sim8, the data-driven Probabilistic Response Simulator, preserved the overlapping dwell ranges observed in real VR-based museum visitor data: confusion responses produced dwell values in the range [0.25, 0.50], while question responses produced dwell in [0.40, 0.70]. The overlap region [0.40, 0.50] meant that a dwell signal of 0.45 was ambiguous — it could indicate either confusion or moderate curiosity. Under Sim8, flat Actor-Critic policies achieved high coverage (98.7%) by exploiting novelty rewards, but used AskQuestion actions only 4.4% of the time and exhibited no explicit recovery behaviour, because the ambiguous signals prevented the agent from learning the association between engagement drops and corrective actions. The State Machine simulator, by contrast, used non-overlapping dwell ranges with a 0.20 gap between positive states (minimum 0.75) and negative states (maximum 0.55), enabling flat policies to learn recovery strategies (35.8% recovery action usage, 31.7% AskQuestion) — behaviours that were fundamentally impossible under Sim8's ambiguous signals.

Shi and Yu's framework thus provides the theoretical lens through which to interpret Bourdon's results: signal quality determines not merely how fast agents learn, but what behaviours they can learn at all. The present thesis extends this insight by asking a more fine-grained question: if signal quality matters, does it matter in a graded fashion? That is, can one construct a sequence of simulators with progressively increasing fidelity and observe a corresponding progression in the richness of learned policies? Shi and Yu compared simulator variants but did not construct a controlled fidelity ladder; the present work aims to fill this gap through the SM-v1 → SM-v4 progression.

## 2.1.3 Alternative Paradigms: Inverse Reinforcement Learning for User Simulation

A complementary approach to simulator design draws on inverse reinforcement learning (IRL), in which the simulator's behaviour policy is not hand-crafted but inferred from observed human–agent interactions. Chandramohan et al. (2011) proposed using IRL to learn a user simulation model for dialogue systems, framing the problem as recovering a reward function that rationalises observed user behaviour and then using the recovered reward to generate simulated user actions. The key advantage of IRL-based simulation is that it sidesteps the need to manually specify transition probabilities or response distributions: if sufficient interaction data is available, the simulator's dynamics can be learned directly from human behaviour traces.

The IRL approach addresses a specific weakness of hand-crafted simulators: the risk that the designer's assumptions about user behaviour may not match actual user patterns. In agenda-based simulators (Li et al., 2016), the designer must specify how the user responds to each system action, which slot values the user prioritises, and what error patterns are plausible. These specifications are necessarily approximate, and mismatches between the specified and actual user behaviour can produce policies that fail at deployment — exactly the concern raised by Schatzmann et al. (2007). IRL-based simulation offers a principled alternative: by learning the user policy from data, it can capture patterns that a human designer might not anticipate.

However, IRL-based simulation carries its own limitations that are particularly relevant to the museum guide context. First, it requires a corpus of human–agent interactions of sufficient size and diversity to recover a meaningful reward function. In museum settings, such corpora are scarce: Bourdon's (2025) Sim8 was trained on VR-based interactions from a single exhibition setting (Dutch Golden Age portraits), and even this dataset was limited in the range of visitor types and engagement patterns it captured. Second, IRL recovers a reward function that rationalises *observed* behaviour, which may not be the same as the reward function that produces *optimal* engagement. If the observed visitors were mostly disengaged or followed suboptimal information-seeking strategies, the recovered reward function would reproduce those patterns rather than the idealised engagement dynamics that a museum guide agent should be trained to manage. Third, the computational cost of IRL — which involves solving an RL problem in an inner loop for each iteration of reward recovery — makes it impractical for the rapid prototyping cycle required when iterating on simulator designs, as the present thesis proposes.

These considerations suggest that IRL-based simulation is most valuable as a validation tool (checking whether a hand-crafted simulator's dynamics are consistent with observed human behaviour) rather than as the primary approach to simulator construction in data-scarce engagement-driven domains. The present work therefore follows Bourdon's approach of using theoretically motivated, hand-crafted simulators, but addresses the concern about ecological validity by systematically varying fidelity dimensions and measuring their effects on policy learning.

## 2.1.4 Empirical Evidence: Simulator Realism and Policy Quality

While Shi and Yu (2019) established the conceptual framework for understanding simulator design as a policy-quality determinant, Kreyssig et al. (2023) provided direct empirical evidence that increasing simulator realism causes measurable improvements in downstream dialogue policy performance. Working in the context of adversarial training for neural user simulators, they demonstrated that a more realistic simulator — one whose behaviour distribution more closely matched human conversational patterns — produced dialogue policies that achieved an 8.3% higher task success rate compared to policies trained against a less realistic baseline simulator. This result is significant because it establishes a causal relationship (within the experimental setup) between simulator fidelity and policy quality, moving beyond the correlational observations that characterise much of the simulator design literature.

The adversarial training approach of Kreyssig et al. is instructive for the present work, though for different reasons than its authors intended. Their key insight was that the simulator and the dialogue policy exist in a co-adaptive relationship: as the policy improves, it encounters dialogue states that the simulator may not model well, and these modelling gaps become the binding constraint on further policy improvement. By training the simulator adversarially — updating it to produce interactions that are maximally challenging for the current policy while remaining consistent with observed human behaviour — they ensured that the simulator's fidelity was highest precisely where it mattered most for policy learning. This co-adaptive perspective resonates with the present thesis's observation that simulator limitations are not uniformly distributed: specific fidelity gaps (such as the absence of cross-exhibit memory, or the lack of temporal fatigue drift) may matter little when the agent is still learning basic strategies but become critical bottlenecks once simpler behaviours have been mastered.

The 8.3% improvement reported by Kreyssig et al. was measured in a task-completion setting where success is binary. In engagement-driven dialogue, where the outcome is a continuous engagement trajectory rather than a binary success/failure, the effects of simulator fidelity may be both more pervasive and harder to quantify. A more realistic simulator does not merely change the probability of task success; it reshapes the entire reward landscape, potentially enabling entirely new classes of behaviour (as Bourdon's State Machine results demonstrate with the emergence of recovery strategies). The present thesis therefore extends the Kreyssig et al. finding to a continuous-engagement setting, asking whether the relationship between simulator fidelity and policy quality is graded and whether it manifests qualitatively (new behavioural categories emerging at higher fidelity levels) rather than merely quantitatively (the same behaviours executed more reliably).

## 2.1.5 Survey Context: The Broader Landscape

The relationship between simulator design and policy learning is situated within a broader research landscape on reinforcement learning for task-oriented dialogue. Kwan et al. (2023) provide a comprehensive survey of recent advances and challenges in RL methods for dialogue policy learning, identifying simulator design as one of several key factors that determine whether RL-based dialogue systems achieve their theoretical potential. Their survey documents a recurring pattern: promising algorithmic advances (e.g., in policy gradient methods, hierarchical RL, or reward shaping) often fail to deliver their expected benefits when the training simulator does not produce sufficiently structured feedback. This pattern aligns with Bourdon's (2025) finding that reward augmentation failed to improve Option-Critic performance — not because the rewards were poorly designed, but because the simulator's ambiguous signals prevented the architecture from effectively using the reward information it received.

Kwan et al. also identify sample efficiency as a critical challenge for RL-based dialogue systems, noting that dialogue interactions are expensive to collect and that simulators must therefore be designed to maximise the information content of each simulated interaction. This observation connects directly to the rationale for the present thesis's fidelity ladder: by systematically removing ambiguities and adding realistic dynamics, each successive simulator version aims to increase the information content per interaction, enabling the agent to learn richer behaviours from the same number of training episodes.

Weisz et al. (2018) address a related challenge — large action spaces in dialogue systems — and demonstrate that sample-efficient deep RL methods can mitigate this problem when the training environment provides sufficiently discriminative feedback. Their work on Bayesian action selection for dialogue complements the simulator fidelity argument: even with sophisticated algorithms for handling large action spaces, the learning signal must be clear enough for the algorithm to discriminate between effective and ineffective actions. In Bourdon's museum dialogue domain, the action space includes 8–12 primitive actions (ExplainNewFact, ClarifyFact, AskQuestion, OfferTransition, etc.) with dynamic availability (actions become masked when exhibits are exhausted). Under Sim8's ambiguous signals, the agent could not distinguish the engagement effects of these actions, leading to the 88.3% option collapse onto ExplainNewFact observed in Option-Critic policies. Sample-efficient algorithms alone cannot solve this problem if the underlying feedback signal is ambiguous.

## 2.1.6 Daniel's Simulators: Positioning and Limitations

Bourdon's (2025) thesis implemented two simulators that represent distinct points on the fidelity–learnability trade-off. Sim8, the Probabilistic Response Simulator, was derived from a prior VR-based museum visitor simulator trained on real interaction data. It preserved empirically observed response type distributions (question, statement, acknowledgment, confusion, follow-up) with persona-specific tendencies (Agreeable, Conscientious, Neurotic visitors based on Big Five traits) and synthesised six gaze features per turn (scanpath length, saccade span, gaze entropy, fixation change rate, dominant object ratio, entry latency). Its primary limitation was the overlapping dwell ranges documented in Table 6 of Bourdon's thesis: confusion responses ([0.25, 0.50]) overlapped with question responses ([0.40, 0.70]) and statement responses ([0.30, 0.60]), creating a region of ambiguity in which the same dwell value could indicate markedly different engagement states.

The State Machine simulator was designed specifically to test whether clearer signals would produce qualitatively different policies. It modelled visitor engagement as a nine-state finite-state machine (ranging from HighlyEngaged to Disengaged) with deterministic triggers, non-overlapping dwell ranges, explicit recovery mechanisms with defined success rates, and anti-spam penalties that made exploitative strategies unprofitable. The State Machine's results were striking: flat Actor-Critic policies achieved 35.8% recovery action usage and 31.7% AskQuestion usage under State Machine, compared to 0% explicit recovery and 4.4% AskQuestion under Sim8 — a 7.2-fold increase in question-asking behaviour (Bourdon, 2025, §9.4.3). These results provided the strongest evidence in Bourdon's thesis that simulator design is a fundamental bottleneck, not merely an implementation detail.

However, both simulators have known limitations that the reviewed literature helps to contextualise. Five specific gaps are relevant to the present work:

First, *overlapping dwell ranges* (Sim8) or *non-overlapping but deterministic ranges* (State Machine) represent the two extremes of a spectrum. Real visitor engagement signals are neither as ambiguous as Sim8's overlapping distributions nor as crisp as State Machine's deterministic boundaries. Shi and Yu's (2019) emphasis on dynamic range suggests that the optimal simulator would provide separable but stochastic engagement signals — distinguishable on average, but with realistic per-turn variability.

Second, *binary state transitions* in the State Machine (a visitor is either ENGAGED or CONFUSED, switching instantaneously) do not capture the gradual nature of engagement drift. Real visitors do not transition discretely between states; they exhibit progressive attention decline, gradual onset of fatigue, and fluctuating curiosity that Falk and Dierking (2016) describe as characteristic of the museum experience. The absence of temporal dynamics means that the agent is never required to detect *trends* in engagement, only absolute levels.

Third, *no cross-exhibit memory* in either simulator means that the visitor's response to Exhibit 3 is independent of their experience at Exhibits 1 and 2. In reality, visitors accumulate fatigue, develop thematic interests, and form expectations based on prior exhibits (Bitgood, 2013). A simulator without cross-exhibit memory cannot teach the agent to adapt its pacing or content depth based on the visitor's cumulative experience.

Fourth, *no persona-specific dynamics* in the State Machine means that all visitors respond identically to the same agent actions. While Sim8 incorporated persona variation through Big Five traits, this variation affected only response type probabilities and base dwell ranges, not the underlying engagement dynamics. Chandramohan et al.'s (2011) IRL approach suggests that individual variation in engagement dynamics (not just surface-level response distributions) is a significant source of real-world complexity.

Fifth, *no temporal fatigue drift* in either simulator means that engagement does not decline naturally over time unless the agent takes explicitly harmful actions (e.g., spamming facts). In real museum visits, attention follows a well-documented trajectory of initial engagement followed by gradual decline, with periodic re-engagement driven by novelty or interactivity (Falk and Dierking, 2018). A simulator without temporal drift cannot train agents to manage the natural arc of a museum visit.

These five limitations define the dimensions of the simulator improvement ladder (SM-v1 → SM-v4) proposed in this thesis, where each version addresses one or more of these gaps while maintaining the learnability properties that Shi and Yu (2019) identified as essential.

## 2.1.7 Synthesis: The Gap This Thesis Fills

The literature reviewed in this section establishes three converging conclusions. First, simulator design is a first-order determinant of dialogue policy quality, not an ancillary engineering concern. This is demonstrated theoretically by Shi and Yu (2019), empirically by Kreyssig et al. (2023), and practically by Bourdon's (2025) comparison of Sim8 and State Machine. Second, the existing literature has investigated the *existence* of the simulator fidelity–policy quality relationship but has not systematically investigated its *structure*: no prior work has constructed a controlled fidelity ladder and measured the incremental effect of each fidelity improvement on learned behaviour. Shi and Yu compared simulator variants but did not control for individual design dimensions; Kreyssig et al. compared realistic versus less realistic simulators but treated fidelity as a unitary construct; Bourdon compared two simulators at opposite ends of the fidelity spectrum but did not investigate intermediate points. Third, the specific fidelity dimensions that matter for engagement-driven dialogue (temporal dynamics, cross-exhibit memory, persona-specific engagement trajectories, stochastic but separable signal ranges) are identified by the literature but have not been systematically manipulated in an RL training context.

The present thesis addresses this gap directly. By constructing a simulator improvement ladder — progressing from SM-v1 (baseline State Machine with minor corrections) through SM-v2 (stochastic engagement bands), SM-v3 (temporal fatigue drift and cross-exhibit memory), to SM-v4 (persona-specific engagement dynamics) — and training identical RL agents at each level, this work provides the first controlled investigation of how graded simulator fidelity improvements affect the emergence of engagement-adaptive dialogue policies. This design answers Shi and Yu's implicit question (does fidelity improvement yield graded policy improvement?) and extends Kreyssig et al.'s finding (that realism drives policy quality) from binary task-completion to continuous engagement maximisation. In doing so, it responds to Bourdon's (2025, §9.4) central finding that signal quality fundamentally determines what behaviours agents can learn — by asking, for the first time, precisely *how much* signal quality is needed to enable *which* behaviours.
